Building Software with AI — Without Losing Control

An introduction to spec-driven AI development: write down what you want before the AI writes code, and approve the work at explicit gates.

~1 MIN — Set expectations: no prior agentic-coding experience assumed. Promise: what spec-driven development is, why it beats just prompting, how the process works step by step, and a real internal service built this way.
First, some vocabulary

Two ways to build with AI

Prompt-driven ("vibe coding")

  • Describe what you want in a chat message.
  • The AI writes code immediately.
  • You react to what comes back and prompt again.
  • Requirements live in your head and the chat scrollback.

Spec-driven

  • Write down what to build in a short document first.
  • The AI critiques it, asks questions, then plans against it.
  • Code gets written in small approved steps.
  • Requirements and decisions live in files in the repo.
~1.5 MIN — Define both without judgment first. Vibe coding is genuinely great for prototypes and one-offs — say so. The question is what happens when the work is real: multi-day, reviewed, maintained.
Why prompting alone breaks down

The failure modes of "just prompt it"

  • Code before clarity. Implementation starts before anyone knows the actual requirements.
  • Silent decisions. The AI picks an architecture, a library, a data model — and nobody approved it.
  • Scope drift. Each prompt nudges the code somewhere new; nothing anchors it.
  • "Looks plausible" gets merged. Confident-sounding output is not the same as correct output.
  • The reasoning evaporates. Why did we do it this way? The answer lived in a chat that's gone.
~1.5 MIN — These will land even with people who've only used AI casually — most have seen "confidently wrong" output. The last bullet sets up the files idea later: chat is a terrible system of record.
The fix, part one

A spec turns prompting into engineering

  • Forces clarity before code. Writing half a page of "what and why" surfaces the questions you didn't know you had.
  • Gives the AI boundaries. Goals, constraints, and explicit non-goals — instead of guessing your intent.
  • Gives the reviewer a yardstick. Code is judged against the spec, not against "seems fine."
  • Outlives the conversation. The spec is a file in the repo — the record of what we meant to build.

A spec here is half a page to two pages of markdown: objective, constraints, non-goals, acceptance criteria. Not a 40-page requirements document.

~1 MIN — Kill the waterfall worry immediately: the spec is short, it's allowed to change, and changing it is explicit rather than accidental. "Non-goals" is worth calling out — telling the AI what NOT to build is half the value.
The fix, part two

The human gate keeps it under control

GATE 1

The spec

You approve what's being built before any planning.

GATE 2

The plan

You approve the phases and sequencing before any code.

GATE 3

Each phase

You review every increment — with tests passing — before the next.

GATE 4

Final review

You approve the whole result before calling it done.

The AI prepares work for review; the human approves, commits, and ships. Without gates, AI moves quickly in the wrong direction. With gates, speed becomes trustworthy.

~1.5 MIN — The core reassurance for skeptics: the AI never runs ahead of your understanding. Nothing merges because it "looks plausible" — every gate is a human saying "yes, I understand this, continue."

Step by step · 1 of 4

The spec HUMAN

# Add CSV Export for Admin Reports

## Objective
Admins can export the current
report view as CSV.

## Constraints
- must respect active filters
- admins only

## Non-Goals
- no scheduled exports, no PDF

## Acceptance Criteria
- export matches filtered table
- non-admins never see the action
  • Written by the developer — this is the thinking step, and it stays human.
  • Doesn't need to be perfect; needs to make the intent legible.
  • Non-goals are load-bearing: they stop the AI from "helpfully" building extra.
  • Lives at specs/001-csv-export-spec.md.
~1 MIN — Emphasize how small this is — half a page. The audience should think "I could write that in 15 minutes." Because they could.

or run in the background?

> Decision:          (human answers)
> Async over 10k rows. We hit
> this exact timeout in reporting.

> Question:          (still open)

  • The AI reads the spec and writes its questions to a file, not into chat.
  • You answer in your editor, on your own time — think, revise, then continue.
  • > Decision: answers get folded into the spec; open items roll into the next round.
  • Six months later, why we chose async is still on disk.
~1.5 MIN — The AI is genuinely good at finding ambiguity — often better than a human reviewer, because it has no shared assumptions to paper over gaps. And the record of decisions is something even non-AI processes rarely produce.

  • The AI drafts the plan; you approve it before any code — reorder, split, cut speculative work.
  • Then it implements one phase only — never ahead "while it's there."
  • A phase is done when the full test suite passes and you approve — evidence, not confidence.
  • Live checklist: [ ] todo  [-] doing  [x] done  [!] blocked, with the reason.
~1 MIN — Two things to stress: the human shapes the plan before any code exists, and bounded phases keep every review small enough to actually review. The next slide covers what happens when a phase finishes.
Step by step · 4 of 4

Closing out a phase — and the work HUMAN

  • Phase review. With tests green, you review the phase for correctness, scope, and design fit — approve it or send it back for another pass.
  • Record and commit. On approval the AI writes the phase review and retro files; you make a checkpoint commit. If the next phase goes sideways, you roll back cheaply.
  • Repeat until every phase in the plan is complete.
  • Final review. One pass across the whole implementation against the spec and plan — cross-phase issues, cleanup, docs. Bounded improvement passes repeat until you approve.
  • Ship. Merge the finished branch into main on GitHub. The merge is yours, like every other git operation.
~1 MIN — This closes the loop slide 9 opened: implement → validate → review → record → commit → next phase; then final review → merge. The rhythm to convey: every approved phase is a durable, committed checkpoint — progress never depends on a live AI session.
Why it all lives in files

The paper trail is the product too

specs/
  001-csv-export-spec.md            ← what we're building, and why
  001-csv-export-questions-01.md    ← every decision, with reasoning
  001-csv-export-plan.md            ← phases + current status
  001-csv-export-phase-01-review.md ← what was approved, per phase
  001-csv-export-phase-01-retro.md  ← what we'd do differently
  • Pick up where you left off — tomorrow, next sprint, or after the AI session dies mid-feature.
  • Anyone can take over — a teammate, a different AI tool, a fresh session. The intent is on disk, not in someone's chat history.
  • Reviews get easier — the PR comes with the spec, the decisions, and the plan that produced it.
~1 MIN — This answers "why files and not just better prompting." Chat is ephemeral and single-player; files are durable and shared. Same reason we don't keep requirements in Slack threads.

Fitting it to the work

Match the weight to the task

WorkProcess weight
Tiny fix / copy changeShort spec → quick AI critique → 2–4 step plan → implement. Minutes of overhead.
Medium featureClarification loop until the spec is ready, phased plan, gated implementation.
High-risk / multi-weekFull artifact set: numbered files, phase reviews, retros, checkpoint commits.

At every weight the same three principles hold: clarify before coding, bound the scope, require evidence before "done."

~1 MIN — Defuses "this seems heavy." A one-line patch doesn't need a spec, and the process says so itself. The full version is for work that has to survive days, handoffs, and review.
Takeaway

Write it down first.
Approve it in steps.
